### 🏛️ Texas Supreme Court: Harris County Deportation Defense Funding Ordered Frozen

The **Supreme Court of the United States** and state high courts are actively policing the boundaries of municipal spending. In a major victory for state-level fiscal controls, Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton successfully secured an emergency freeze from the **Supreme Court of Texas (SCOTX)** halting a controversial localized immigration defense fund.

- **The Dispute:** Harris County unlawfully directed over $1.3 million in local taxpayer funds to establish a public program explicitly designed to provide legal defense representation to undocumented individuals facing federal deportation proceedings.
- **The Ruling:** SCOTX intervened directly, issuing a formal order that bars Harris County from continuing to disburse or allocate public funds into the program while the underlying constitutional litigation proceeds.
- **The Legal Logic:** The state successfully argued that local municipalities lack the statutory authority to divert taxpayer resources to actively interfere with or defend against federal immigration enforcement actions.
- **The Practice Takeaway:** For municipal, administrative, and corporate counsel, this order underscores a strict zero-tolerance environment for local government entities attempting to create independent immigration or regulatory policy. Expect the high court to continue to tightly enforce statutory limits on public fund distribution as this case moves to a final merits review.

### 🏡 Oklahoma Property Law: Strict Limitations Block Trailing Foreclosure Tracks

Real estate litigators, financial institutional counsel, and title examiners across Oklahoma must log a vital new enforcement standard handed down by the **Oklahoma Supreme Court** (*RCB Bank v. Stitt*, 2026 OK 49). The decision completely reshapes multi-county debt collection and collateral recovery limits.

- **The Scenario:** A commercial lender attempted to foreclose on real estate mortgages in Tulsa County. However, the underlying promissory note liability had already been heavily litigated and reduced to a formal deficiency judgment in a separate, prior foreclosure action located across the county line in Washington County.
- **The Appellate Turn:** The Court of Civil Appeals originally ruled in favor of the bank, holding that entering a deficiency judgment effectively "revives" the baseline statute of limitations on notes and actively prevents subsequent mortgage liens from being extinguished.
- **The High Court Reversal:** The state Supreme Court officially vacated the lower order. The High Court clarified that once a lender reduces a note to a definitive deficiency judgment in one jurisdiction, they cannot use parallel, trailing multi-county property actions to artificially expand state statute of limitations constraints or circumvent the strict statutory timeline mandates governing lien extinguishment under **42 O.S. § 23**.
- **The Strategic Takeaway:** Transactional and bankruptcy practitioners can weaponize this precedent immediately. When a creditor group splits an asset workout or recovery strategy across multiple Oklahoma counties, any failure to preserve parallel timelines will result in an absolute statutory bar against outstanding real estate liens.
