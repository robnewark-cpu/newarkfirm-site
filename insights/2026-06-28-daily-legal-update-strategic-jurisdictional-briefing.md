Supreme Court of Texas: Harris County Deportation Defense Funding Ordered Frozen

The boundary lines of municipal authority and local tax allocations have been heavily reinforced by the state's highest civil court. Following a direct intervention by Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton, the **Supreme Court of Texas** issued an emergency order halting a localized immigration defense framework.

- The Dispute: Attorney General Paxton initiated a lawsuit against Harris County, alleging the municipality acted unlawfully by directing over 1.3 million dollars in local taxpayer funds to establish a public program explicitly designed to provide legal representation to undocumented individuals facing federal deportation proceedings.
- The Mandate: The court granted the state's request, entering a formal order that explicitly bars Harris County from continuing to disburse or allocate public funds into the program while the underlying constitutional litigation proceeds.
- The Legal Principle: The state successfully argued that local county governments lack the foundational statutory authority to divert taxpayer resources to actively interfere with or defend against federal immigration enforcement dockets.
- The Practice Takeaway: Corporate, administrative, and municipal practitioners must note this zero-tolerance stance toward local government entities attempting to create independent immigration policy. Corporate counsel should advise municipal clients that the high court is strictly enforcing statutory limits on public fund distribution as this case continues to be litigated.

Supreme Court of Texas: Sovereign Immunity Bars Uvalde Negligence Claims

In an emotionally charged order issued without comment, the **Supreme Court of Texas** officially rejected an appeal brought by 20 students and five teachers who survived the 2022 Robb Elementary School mass shooting in Uvalde.

- The Core Pleading: The families sued the Texas Department of Public Safety and Uvalde County, arguing that severe tactical delays and operational communication breakdowns contributed directly to the scale of the tragedy. The plaintiffs alleged that law enforcement officers' failure to use available body armor, ballistics shields, and communication gear directly aggravated the situation.
- The Holding: The High Court let a lower court dismissal stand, reinforcing the ironclad scope of **governmental sovereign immunity**.
- The Structural Barrier: The court affirmed the long-standing Texas rule that under the Texas Tort Claims Act, the **non-use of government property does not constitute a waiver of immunity**. Because the statute strictly requires an injury to be directly caused by a defect or the active, negligent use of state property, tactical omissions and operational delays do not unlock a path to sue state entities. The court reiterated that expanding waivers of immunity is a policy matter belonging strictly to the Texas Legislature, not the judiciary.

Environmental Law: Multi-Decade Illinois River Watershed Litigation Hits Stalemate

Real estate, environmental, and agribusiness counsel across Oklahoma are analyzing the fallout of an ongoing procedural gridlock in the state's long-running agricultural pollution case pending before **U.S. District Judge Gregory Frizzell**.

- The Historical Context: Initiated over 20 years ago by former Oklahoma Attorney General Drew Edmondson, the state sued 14 major poultry companies, including Tyson Foods, alleging that bird waste runoff heavily contaminated the scenic Illinois River Watershed with high levels of phosphorus.
- The Current Battle: While a 2023 ruling found the poultry companies responsible, recent efforts by select companies to execute a less expensive, negotiated cleanup plan with state officials have completely broken down. Judge Frizzell formally rejected the proposed settlements, leaving both environmental advocates and agricultural producers in a prolonged state of legal limbo.
- The Corporate Impact: Tyson Foods has indicated it will not renew grower contracts in the watershed unless the settlements go through, signaling extreme economic pressure on local agricultural operations as multiple appeals remain pending.
